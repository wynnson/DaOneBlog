Bayes Thereom

Probability vs Likelihood

Before getting started, we need to understand probability vs likelihood:

  • Probability: Chance of future outcomes based on known distribution or parameters
  • Likelihood: How probable the outcome is to fit our parameters

At a high level, Bayes Thereom uses prior probability and the likelihood of it to calculate new probability (posterior).

Bayes Thereom

$$ P(A | B) = \frac{P(B | A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} $$ $P(A | B)$ = Posterior (what is the probability of case A given B?)
$P(B | A)$ = Likelihood
$P(A)$ = Probability of A (Prior)
$P(B)$ = Probability of B (Marginalization)
Range: [0, 1]

Note

You might find $P(A \cap B) = P(A, \ B)$ which is the same thing. Also, if you solve for $P(B|A)$, notice how Bayes is symmetric. By that same symmetry: $$ P(A \cap B) = P(A | B) \cdot P(B) = P(B | A) \cdot P(A) $$

The entire point is to update your posterior belief as with your prior belief. Your posterior will then become your prior. The prior is something that you come up with. It could be from a distribution or by elite ball knowledge.

More importantly since we are working with mainly conditional probabilities, the marginalization or $P(B)$ uses the law of total probability.

Law of Total Probability

$$ P(B) = \sum^k_{i=1} P(B | A_i) \cdot P(A_i) $$
Source: GeeksforGeeks

The probability of B occuring is the probability of B over all cases of A.

However, Bayes is not only just dependent on just A and B. We have have a bunch of variables. $$ P(A \cap B \cap C \cap D \cap \dots) $$ To handle this, we can use the chain rule of probability:

Chain Rule of Probability

$$ P(A, B, C) = P(A, B | C) \cdot P(C) = P(A | B, C) \cdot P(B, C) \cdot P(C) $$
You might wonder how we got from $P(A, B | C)$ to $P(A | B, C) \cdot P(B, C)$. Here is a small workthrough: $$ \begin{aligned} P(A, B | C) &= \frac{P(A, B, C)}{P(C)} \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \text{Def. of Bayes} \\ P(A, B, C) &= P(A | B, C) \cdot P(B, C) \qquad \qquad \qquad \ \text{Chain Rule} \\ P(B, C) &= P(B | C) \cdot P(C) \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \ \ \ \text{Chain Rule} \\ P(A, B | C) &= \frac{P(A | B, C) P(B | C) P(C)}{P(C)} \\ P(A, B | C) &= P(A | B, C) P(B | C) \qquad \qquad \qquad \ \ \ \ \ \text{Cancel $P(C)$} \\ \end{aligned} $$

Note

The permutations are the same $$P(A, B, C) = P(B, A, C) = P(C, B, A) = P(A, C, B) \dots$$

Note on Cardinalities

  • $|$Input Rows$|$ = mulitply cardinalities of parent blocks
  • $|$Input Cols$|$ = number of parents
  • $|$Output Cols$|$ = $|$child$|$
  • $|$Total Cols$|$ = $|$Input Cols$|$ + $|$Output Cols$|$

Information flow can be described as knowing belief of one variable helps update your belief about another one. There are typically 3 types of information flow.

  • Pipe (A -> B -> C): If we don't know the middle (B), then we can use A tell us about B (information flows). However, the moment you know B, you don't need A (*).

  • Fork (A <- B -> C): If we don't know the middle (B), then we can use A to tell us about C, vice versa (information flows). However, the moment you know B, you don't need A / C (*).

  • Collider (A -> B <- C): If we don't know B, A and C tell us nothing about each other (no flow) but the moment we do, there is information flow (*).
Notice that these tell you about possible conditional independence.

Conditional Independence (*)

$X$ and $Y$ are conditionally independent given $Z$ and $Y$ if $Y$ gives no additional information about $X$ if we know $Z$. In other words $P(X | Y, Z)$ = $P(X | Z)$ if conditionally independent since knowing $Z$ means we can discard $Y$.

You pick a random rat from the group of rats that have the worm and are hot. What is the probability that it also has the virus? $$ \begin{aligned} P(V{=}t \mid W{=}t, H{=}t) &= \frac{P(V{=}t, W{=}t, H{=}t)}{P(W{=}t, H{=}t)} \\[4pt] P(V{=}t, W{=}t, H{=}t) &= P(H{=}t \mid W{=}t, V{=}t)P(W{=}t \mid V{=}t)P(V{=}t) \\[4pt] P(W{=}t) &= P(W{=}t \mid M{=}t)P(M{=}t) + P(W{=}t \mid M{=}f)P(M{=}f) \\[4pt] &= (0.15)(0.3) + (0.01)(0.7) = 0.052 \\[4pt] P(V{=}t, W{=}t, H{=}t) &= (0.85)(0.052)(0.06) = 0.002652 \\[4pt] P(W{=}t, H{=}t) &= P(H{=}t \mid W{=}t)P(W{=}t) \\[4pt] P(H{=}t \mid W{=}t) &= P(H{=}t, V{=}t \mid W{=}t) + P(H{=}t, V{=}f \mid W{=}t) \\[4pt] &= P(H{=}t \mid V{=}t, W{=}t)P(V{=}t \mid W{=}t) + P(H{=}t \mid V{=}f, W{=}t)P(V{=}f \mid W{=}t) \\[4pt] &= (0.85)(0.06) + (0.8)(0.94) = 0.803 \\[4pt] P(W{=}t, H{=}t) &= (0.803)(0.052) = 0.041756 \\[4pt] P(V{=}t \mid W{=}t, H{=}t) &= \frac{0.002652}{0.041756} \approx 0.0635 \end{aligned} $$
3. You pick a random rat from the group of rats that are not hot yet are unresponsive. What is the probability that it has the virus? $$ \begin{aligned} P(V{=}t \mid U{=}t, H{=}f) &= \frac{P(U{=}t, H{=}f, V{=}t)}{P(H{=}f, U{=}t)} \\[4pt] P(U{=}t, H{=}f, V{=}t) &= P(U{=}t \mid H{=}f, V{=}t)P(H{=}f \mid V{=}t)P(V{=}t) \\[4pt] P(U{=}t \mid H{=}f, V{=}t) &= P(U{=}t, C{=}t \mid H{=}f, V{=}t) + P(U{=}t, C{=}f \mid H{=}f, V{=}t) \\[4pt] &= P(U{=}t \mid C{=}t, H{=}f)P(C{=}t \mid V{=}t) + P(U{=}t \mid C{=}f, H{=}f)P(C{=}f \mid V{=}t) \\[4pt] &= (0.4)(0.75) + (0.3)(0.25) = 0.375 \\[4pt] P(H{=}f \mid V{=}t) &= P(H{=}f \mid W{=}t, V{=}t)P(W{=}t) + P(H{=}f \mid W{=}f, V{=}t)P(W{=}f) \\[4pt] P(W{=}t) &= P(W{=}t \mid M{=}t)P(M{=}t) + P(W{=}t \mid M{=}f)P(M{=}f) \\[4pt] &= (0.15)(0.3) + (0.01)(0.7) = 0.052, \quad P(W{=}f)=0.948 \\[4pt] P(H{=}f \mid V{=}t) &= (0.15)(0.052) + (0.45)(0.948) = 0.4344 \\[4pt] P(U{=}t, H{=}f, V{=}t) &= (0.375)(0.4344)(0.06) = 0.009774 \\[4pt] P(H{=}f, U{=}t) &= P(H{=}f, U{=}t, V{=}t) + P(H{=}f, U{=}t, V{=}f) \\[4pt] P(H{=}f, U{=}t, V{=}f) &= P(U{=}t \mid H{=}f, V{=}f)P(H{=}f \mid V{=}f)P(V{=}f) \\[4pt] P(U{=}t \mid H{=}f, V{=}f) &= (0.4)(0.3) + (0.3)(0.7) = 0.33 \\[4pt] P(H{=}f \mid V{=}f) &= (0.2)(0.052) + (0.95)(0.948) = 0.911 \\[4pt] P(H{=}f, U{=}t, V{=}f) &= (0.33)(0.911)(0.94) = 0.2825922 \\[4pt] P(H{=}f, U{=}t) &= 0.009774 + 0.2825922 = 0.2923662 \\[4pt] P(V{=}t \mid U{=}t, H{=}f) &= \frac{0.009774}{0.2923662} \approx 0.0334 \end{aligned} $$ Notice how marginalizing and summing across hidden variables (sandwiched variables) requires a bunch of extra steps. This becomes very computationally intensive. Thus, Bayes Nets are good for small datasets. It would be easier to assume conditional independence and remove a lot of these terms. This is why Brandon chose Naive Bayes.

Naive Bayes Classifiers - Spam Filter

One of the problems with Bayes Nets was that it was too computationally expensive to calculate all the terms. Instead we can assume features are condtionally independent.

Naive Bayes

We assume conditional independence aka the "naive" assumption.

$$ P(C \mid x_1,\dots,x_n)\propto P(C)\prod_{i=1}^{n}P(x_i\mid C) $$

For numeric / continuous data, we can model each feature with a distribution such as a gaussian distribution in the multivariate case (use $\sigma^2$ for the variance in 1D gaussian): $$ \mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \\ \Sigma = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \mu)(x_i - \mu)^T \quad \text{Covariance Matrix}\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{(2\pi)^d |\Sigma|}} \exp \left( -\frac{1}{2}(x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu) \right) $$

Note

The covariance matrix: $$ \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_x^2 & \sigma_{xy} \\ \sigma_{xy} & \sigma_y^2 \end{bmatrix} $$

  • Contains variances on the diagonal (controls spread).
  • Contains covariances on the off-diagonal (controls tilt / rotation).
However, here, we are trying to detect words like: job, summer, internship, work. Assuming conditional independence we get: $$ P(\text{spam} | \text{job, summer, internship, work}) \propto P(\text{spam} | \text{job}) P(\text{spam} | \text{summer}) P(\text{spam} | \text{internship}) P(\text{spam} | \text{work}) $$ Notice how this is way simpler since we only depend on one feature at a time. This allows Naive Bayes to do fast spam detection on larger datasets. Naive bayes is also fast to train because it only requires a single pass through the data to calculate the probabilities.
